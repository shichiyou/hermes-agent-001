# Consulting Thinking Framework

## When to Load

Load this skill when:
- Analyzing a problem or making a decision
- Planning strategy or prioritizing options
- Structuring research or investigation
- Breaking down complex requirements
- Any task requiring structured reasoning beyond simple execution

## Overview

Problem-solving consists of two layers:

- **Thinking Methods (Process)** = the flow/recipe: Issue Thinking → Hypothesis Thinking → Strategy Thinking → Tactics Thinking
- **Thinking Tools (Instruments)** = MECE, Fermi estimation, factorization, logic trees, etc.

Use methods as the process, and apply appropriate tools at each stage.

---

## Thinking Process Flow

```
Issue Thinking (Define the question)
    "What should we solve?"
        ↓
Hypothesis Thinking (Tentative answer)
    "What is the likely answer?"
        ↓
Logical Thinking + Factorization (Verification design)
    "Structure the hypothesis into testable elements"
        ↓
Fermi Estimation (Quantitative backing)
    "Estimate the scale numerically"
        ↓
Strategy Thinking (Selection & focus)
    "Where do we concentrate resources?"
        ↓
Tactics Thinking (Execution design)
    "How do we realize the strategy?"
        ↓
Execution → Monitoring → Improvement
```

**Key Principle: The issue is the starting point. No question = no strategy. A wrong question leads to a wrong strategy even if the strategy itself is excellent.**

## Thinking Methods in Detail

### 1. Issue Thinking

| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Question** | "What should we solve?" |
| **Role** | Problem setting, identifying the real question |
| **Output** | The question to solve |

**4-step process**:
1. **Clarify the problem**: Don't jump to How. Start with What.
2. **List candidate issues**: Use MECE to enumerate possibilities.
3. **Prioritize**: Filter by impact, feasibility, speed.
4. **Confirm the issue**: Review causality and overall balance.

### 2. Hypothesis Thinking

| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Question** | "What is the answer?" |
| **Role** | Efficient path to solution |
| **Output** | Hypothesis (tentative answer) |

**Characteristics**:
- Set a tentative conclusion first, then gather only necessary information to prove/revise it.
- If the hypothesis is wrong, revise immediately (trial and error).

### 3. Issue Thinking vs Hypothesis Thinking

| Aspect | Issue Thinking | Hypothesis Thinking |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Question** | What should we solve? | What is the answer? |
| **Role** | Choosing the question | Placing a tentative answer |
| **Order** | First | Second (wrong issue = wasted hypothesis) |

### 4. Strategy Thinking

| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Question** | "Where to win?" "What to focus on?" |
| **Role** | Direction and resource allocation |
| **Output** | Strategy (basic policy) |

**Essence**: Deciding what NOT to do (selection & concentration).

### 5. Tactics Thinking

| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Question** | "How to win?" "How to realize?" |
| **Role** | Execution design |
| **Output** | Execution plan |

| Aspect | Strategy | Tactics |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **View** | Whole-system, mid/long-term | Partial, short-term |
| **Abstraction** | Direction/policy | Concrete actions |

## Thinking Tools

### MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive)

- **Role**: Foundational principle — no overlaps, no gaps.
- **Usage**: All phases. The most basic tool.
- **Example**: Classifying a shopping list as "meat, vegetables, other" — prevents both omissions and duplicates.

### Factorization

- **Role**: Break complex problems into constituent elements.
- **Usage**: After hypothesis thinking (to make it testable).
- **Example**: "ASEAN is promising" → Market size, Growth rate, Competition, Entry barriers, Profitability.

### Fermi Estimation

- **Role**: Logically estimate unknown numbers from available evidence.
- **Usage**: Between hypothesis and strategy (for quantitative backing).
- **Example**: Vietnam EC market = Population 100M × EC-able 30% × EC usage 50% × Annual spend ¥30K = ¥450B.

### Logic Tree

- **Role**: Structure causes or solutions into a MECE tree.
- **Usage**: Issue thinking through hypothesis thinking.

### Tactics Design Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **WBS** | Task breakdown structure |
| **Gantt Chart** | Schedule design (who, when, what) |
| **RACI Matrix** | Responsibility assignment (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) |
| **KPI Design** | Measurable outcome indicators |

## Phase Summary

| Phase | Method | Tools | Question | Output |
|-------|--------|-------|----------|--------|
| Problem setting | Issue thinking | MECE, Issue tree | What to solve? | The question |
| Direction | Hypothesis thinking | Analogy, Abduction | What is the answer? | Hypothesis |
| Structuring | Logical thinking | MECE, Factorization, Logic tree | How to decompose? | Test items |
| Quantification | Fermi estimation | Factorization, Estimation | What is the scale? | Numerical estimates |
| Policy decision | Strategy thinking | 3C, SWOT, 5Forces | Where to focus? | Strategy |
| Execution design | Tactics thinking | WBS, Gantt, RACI, KPI | How to realize? | Execution plan |
| Execution | PDCA/OODA | KPI monitoring | Is it working? | Results & learning |

## 4-Layer Knowledge Model

Understanding what to invest in learning, based on obsolescence speed.

```
【Slow to obsolete】
    ↑
    │  Layer 1: Mental Foundation (MECE awareness, hypothesis orientation...)
    │  Layer 2: Thinking Engine (Issue→Hypothesis→Strategy→Tactics)
    │
────┼──────────────────────────────────────────
    │  Layer 3: Research & Knowledge Update
    │  (Ability persists, methods change)
    │
────┼──────────────────────────────────────────
    │  Layer 4: Weapons & Specific Knowledge
    │    ├── Universal frameworks (MECE, factorization...) — relatively stable
    │    ├── Strategy frameworks (3C, SWOT...) — moderate
    │    ├── Tools & tech (AI, SaaS...) — fast obsolescence
    │    └── Domain knowledge (industry, regulations...) — very fast obsolescence
    ↓
【Fast to obsolete】
```

### Layer Details

| Layer | Name | Definition | Obsolescence | Learning Strategy |
|-------|------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Foundation** | Mental dispositions | Mindset, habits, attitudes | Rarely | Master early — lifelong asset |
| **Engine** | Thinking methods | Process patterns (Issue→Hypothesis→...) | Rarely | Master and refine through practice |
| **Cross-cut** | Research & knowledge update | Information gathering, evaluation | Ability persists, methods change | Build discernment; update tools continuously |
| **Output** | Weapons (tools & knowledge) | Frameworks, domain expertise | Frequently | Update proactively; leverage AI |

## Applying the Framework

### Before Starting Any Analytical Task

1. **Define the issue**: What is the real question? (Issue thinking)
2. **State a hypothesis**: What do I think the answer is? (Hypothesis thinking)
3. **Factorize**: Break the hypothesis into testable elements.
4. **Estimate**: Use Fermi estimation for scale/sense-check.
5. **Choose focus**: Where to concentrate effort? (Strategy thinking)
6. **Design execution**: Specific actions, owners, timeline. (Tactics thinking)

### During Analysis

- Always start from the issue. No issue = no strategy.
- Use MECE at every decomposition step.
- Verify hypotheses with evidence, not wishful thinking.
- When stuck, zoom out (meta-thinking — see `agile-meta-thinking` skill).

### After Analysis

- Document: What was the issue? Hypothesis? Evidence? Conclusion?
- Record rejected alternatives and why.
- Update the next sprint's issue based on learnings.

## Pitfalls

- **Jumping to tactics without an issue**: Solving the wrong problem efficiently.
- **Skipping hypothesis**: Trying to be comprehensive before being directional — paralysis.
- **Confirmation bias in verification**: Only seeking evidence that supports the hypothesis.
- **Using outdated weapons**: Frameworks and tools from years ago may not apply today.
- **Missing MECE**: Gaps or overlaps in analysis lead to blind spots or wasted effort.

## Appendix A — Agile Thinking Cycles & Meta-Thinking

> Use this subsection when a task spans multiple sprints, requires retrospectives, or when you need to monitor the quality of your own reasoning.

### PDCA vs OODA
- **PDCA** (Plan-Do-Check-Act): Best for stable environments with predictable feedback loops.
- **OODA** (Observe-Orient-Decide-Act): Best for fast-changing environments where rapid iteration matters more than perfect planning.

### Hypothesis Verification Loop
1. **Form** — state a falsifiable hypothesis.
2. **Design verification** — choose a minimal experiment.
3. **Run MVP** — execute the smallest test that yields signal.
4. **Learn** — collect evidence, not opinions.
5. **Update or Discard** — revise hypothesis or abandon it.

### Sprint Structure
Each Sprint / Epic / Story / Task runs the full thinking-framework cycle:
- **Sprint Planning** → Issue Thinking + Hypothesis Thinking
- **Sprint Execution** → Strategy Thinking + Tactics Thinking
- **Sprint Review** → Fermi estimation + KPI review
- **Sprint Retrospective** → Meta-cognition + ledger update

### Meta-Cognition Functions
- **Monitoring**: track whether the process is being followed.
- **Evaluation**: assess if current results match expected outcomes.
- **Control**: intervene when execution drifts.

### Retrospective Templates
- **KPT** (Keep / Problem / Try)
- **YWT** (Did / Learned / Will do)
- Apply after every sprint or major milestone.

### Bias Countermeasures
| Bias | Symptom | Countermeasure |
|---|---|---|
| Confirmation bias | Only seeking evidence that supports hypothesis | Explicitly ask: "What would prove me wrong?" |
| Sunk cost | Continuing because of past investment | Ask: "If I started today, would I still choose this?" |
| Anchoring | Over-relying on first data point | Generate 3 independent estimates |
| Overconfidence | Reporting certainty without evidence | Require physical evidence before claiming |

## Appendix B — Mental Models Reference

> A condensed catalog of cross-disciplinary thinking lenses. Use them at specific steps of the framework.

### General Thinking Tools
| Model | Best Applied At | One-Line Summary |
|---|---|---|
| Inversion | Hypothesis Thinking | Instead of asking "How do I succeed?", ask "How do I guarantee failure?" |
| Occam’s Razor | Hypothesis Thinking | The simplest explanation with equal evidence is best. |
| First Principles | Issue Thinking | Break down to fundamental truths; rebuild from there. |
| Second-Order Thinking | Strategy Thinking | Ask: "And then what?" for every proposed action. |
| Map ≠ Territory | Issue Thinking | The model is not the reality; always verify with physical evidence. |
| Circle of Competence | Strategy Thinking | Know the edge of what you actually understand. |

### System Thinking
| Model | Best Applied At | One-Line Summary |
|---|---|---|
| Feedback Loops | Strategy/Tactics | Identify reinforcing or balancing loops before acting. |
| Bottlenecks | Tactics Thinking | Find the constraint; optimize it, not everything else. |
| Margin of Safety | Strategy Thinking | Build in buffers because estimates are wrong. |
| Entropy | Monitoring | Systems naturally drift toward disorder; maintenance is non-optional. |

### Numeracy
| Model | Best Applied At | One-Line Summary |
|---|---|---|
| Power Laws | Issue Thinking | A minority of causes produces the majority of effects. |
| Compounding | Strategy Thinking | Small consistent gains accumulate exponentially. |
| Fermi Estimation | Quantification | Logically estimate unknown numbers from available evidence. |

### Microeconomics & Strategy
| Model | Best Applied At | One-Line Summary |
|---|---|---|
| Opportunity Cost | Strategy Thinking | The cost of the next best alternative foregone. |
| Incentives | Tactics Thinking | People (and agents) do what they are rewarded for. |
| Comparative Advantage | Strategy Thinking | Do what you are least bad at; trade for the rest. |

### Pitfalls
- **Model Overload**: Applying too many lenses to a simple problem creates paralysis.
- **Map ≠ Territory complacency**: Treating a model as gospel without physical verification.
- **Inversion-as-pessimism**: Using inversion only to find problems, not to design robustness.
- **Second-order paralysis**: Spending all your time predicting side effects without acting.
